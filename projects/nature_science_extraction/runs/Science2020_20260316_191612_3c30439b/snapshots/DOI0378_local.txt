Sasidhar5 Received: 15 August 2015 / Accepted: 31 March 2016 / Published online: 12 April 2016  Springer Science+Business Media Dordrecht 2016 Abstract The occurrence of arsenic in drinking water and its detrimental effects have drawn much attention in recent years. Several studies have been conducted in the deltaic plains of River Ganga, NE part of the India, and in other countries, but no systematic study was conducted in South India on occurrence of arsenic in groundwater. The main aim of this study is to determine the level of arsenic in groundwater and to understand the relation with other geochemical parameters of groundwater in the south-eastern coastal aquifer at Kalpakkam region, India. This region is represented by three different lithologies, viz. charnockites, flood plain alluvium and marine alluvium. Twenty-nine representative samples of groundwater were collected and analysed for major ions, metals and isotopes such as 2H and 18O. In addition, geophysical method was also attempted to understand the subsurface condition. The spatial variation in arsenic (As) indicates that higher concen- tration was observed around the landfill sites and irrigated regions, which was supported by geochemical, statistical and isotopic inferences. The variation in the As with depth, lithology and sources has been clearly brought out. Though the values of As does not exceed the drinking water permissible limit (10 mg/l), it has reached a near permissible level of 8.7 ppb. Hence, it is essential to understand the geochemical behaviour of As for a proper future management of the water resource in the study area. This extraction of groundwater resulted in overdrafting, land subsidence, and finally led groundwater quality to be unsuitable for human consumption. It is observed by researchers that the enhanced usage of groundwater resources in countries such as India and Bangladesh has resulted in health impacts on human (Bagla and Kaiser 1996; Kumar 1997; Nickson et al. 1998). It was inferred that about 36 million people are under the hazard of drinking arsenic contaminated in the Bengal Delta. Arsenic being the twentieth most abundant element, it is observed in all the components of the ecosystem such as atmosphere, lithosphere, hydrosphere and biosphere. Arsenic in groundwater is mainly due to the dissolution of ores and minerals in elevated areas due to bed erosion. It may also be contributed by industrial effluents in some areas (Dubey et al. 2012; Oinam et al. 2011). Commercially they are also used as wood preservatives, alloying agents, etc. The release of arsenic to the environment is mainly through combustion of fossil fuels. The health impact on the public is mainly through consumption of water and exposure to hazardous occupation (WHO 2001). The concentration, chemistry source and the variations in arsenic have been studied by different authors (Table 1). It is evident from Table 1 that efforts were undertaken worldwide, but little attention was paid to natural background values in rocks, soils and waters at local scales and this study is one such attempt on groundwater. The present study area has been investigated for different aspects of hydrogeochemistry: quality (Karmegam et al. 2010; Chidambaram et al. 2011), mixing proportion of groundwater (Karmegam et al. 2011), Saturation Index (SI) of clay minerals (Chidambaram et al. 2011) and heavy metal con- centration in groundwater (Chidambaram et al. 2012). The understanding of groundwater system also requires knowledge of stable isotope behaviour in a multifaceted approach (Vengosh et al. 2002; Widory et al. 2004) in order to understand various geochemical controls. In this paper, an attempt has been made to analyse the concentration of arsenic in groundwater of this region, their spatial variation, geochemical nature and source by an integrated approach, using stable isotopes and statistical techniques. 2 Study area The area undertaken for study, Kalpakkam in the Kanchepuram district, comprising hard rock and sedimentary formations located in northern part of Tamil Nadu between 12370 and 12250 north latitudes, 80000 and 80120 east longitudes (Fig. 1). The study area falls in Survey of India toposheet Nos. 66 D 2, 3 and 4 with a total area of 385 km2 bounded in east by Bay of Bengal. The SADRAS backwater is also located in the eastern part along the coast. The landfill sites for the Mahabalipuram Township are also located along the NE. The fluviomarine alluvium and the charnockites are the major litho units. The main river that drains the region is Palar dividing the study area dominated by charnockites into two as southern and northern. The quaternary fluvial alluvium is noted along the river course as it diagonally flows along north-west to south-east. Spatially, marine alluvium is repre- sented along the coast (Fig. 1). Singh et al. (2004) and Singh and Saxena (2004) report that the charnockites form the Archaean basement below the younger alluvium. There are two major aquifers, the weathered/fractured hard rock aquifer and the alluvium. Intervening clays in the alluvium formation are reported in the study area, with more thickness of the formation in the southern and eastern sides (Karmegam et al. 2010). The thickness of these aquifers ranges between 3 and 12 m forming a shallow unconfined aquifer. The chief minerals are quartz, feldspar, biotite and pyroxene. Charnockites are reported to have minerals such as chlorite, altered feldspar and quartz (Gurumoorthy et al. 1994). Shallow open of 6–12 mbgl is common in alluvium and 6–17 mbgl in fissured charnockite. Water-level variations are observed in summer 3.50–8.34 mbgl and post- monsoon 1.32–7.53 mbgl. 3 Methodology 3.1 Hydrochemistry A total of 29 groundwater samples were drawn from three principal aquifer types: (1) unconsolidated flood plain alluvium, (2) marine alluvium and (3) crystalline rock aquifers, forming the major part of the study area (Karmegam 2012). Samples were collected in 1L polythene bottles, and prior to sampling, necessary precautions were taken by cleaning the bottle with the samples. The major ions were analysed by adopting standard procedures (APHA 1995), and the samples were acidified (pH * 2 with HNO3) and preserved at 4 C for further analysis. The samples were then spiked with the standard solution of the metals chosen for analysis. Major cations such as Ca and Mg were analysed by titrimetry, Na and K by flame pho- tometer (Elico CL 378), major anions Cl and HCO3 by titrimetry, and SO4, PO4, NO3 and H4SiO4 by spectrophotometer (SL 171 minispec). The total cation (Tz?) and total anion (Tz-) balance (Domenico and Schwartz 1998) is considered to show the ionic balance error percentage. The error percentage in the samples ranged from ±1 to ±10 %. Fluoride was determined by using Orion fluoride ion selective electrode model (94-09, 96-09). The metal levels in groundwater were measured using inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICPMS) (Model-ELAN DRC II, Perkin Elmer Sciex Instrument).
