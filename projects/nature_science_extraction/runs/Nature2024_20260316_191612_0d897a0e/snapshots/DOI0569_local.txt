ABSTRACT: Data presenting the environmental occurrence of ultra-short-chain perﬂuoroalkyl acids (PFAAs) are scarce and little is known about the potential sources. Samples (n = 34) were collected in connection with ﬁreﬁghting training sites, landﬁlls, and a hazardous waste management facility. Ultra-short-chain PFAAs were detected in all samples at concentrations up to 84 000 ng/L (∑C1−C3), representing up to 69% of the concentration of 29 per- and polyﬂuoroalkyl substances (PFASs). Triﬂuoroacetic acid (TFA), perﬂuor- opropanoic acid (PFPrA), triﬂuoromethane sulfonic acid (TFMS), perﬂuoroethane sulfonic acid (PFEtS), and perﬂuoropropane sulfonic acid (PFPrS) were detected at concentrations up to 14 000, 53 000, 940, 1700, and 15 000 ng/L, respectively. Principal component analysis suggests that TFA is associated with landﬁlls. PFPrS was associated with samples collected close to the source at all types of sites included in this study. These ﬁndings reveal the presence of high concentrations of ultra-short-chain PFAAs released into the environment from various sources and emphasize the large fraction of ultra-short-chain PFAAs to the total concentration of PFASs in water. ■ INTRODUCTION Per- and polyﬂuoroalkyl substances (PFASs) are a group of Convention list on persistent organic pollutants in 2009, restricting its production and use.6 In 2006, eight major highly ﬂuorinated chemicals widely used in diﬀerent industrial companies in the PFASs industry joined a global voluntary and commercial applications since the 1950s because of their stewardship program with the goal to eliminate emissions of unique properties, including surface activity, water and oil PFOA, its related precursors, as well as its related longer-chain repellency, and high stability.
