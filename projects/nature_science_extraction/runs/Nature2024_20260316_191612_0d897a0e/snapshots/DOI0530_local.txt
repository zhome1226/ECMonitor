Tuwima 10, 10-748, Olsztyn, Poland Perﬂuorinated compounds, including perﬂuorinated acids and perﬂuorinated sulfonates, are environ- mentally persistent. These compounds are commonly used in consumer products as stain/water/grease repellents in carpets and clothing, in cooking utensils as nonstick coating and in food contact materials as a coating. This paper explores the potential application of liquid chromatography coupled with tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) system for the measurement of selected perﬂuorinated acids and perﬂuorinated sulfonates in three different brands (A, B and C) of food contact materials, including wrapping papers, breakfast bags, baking papers and roasting bags. In this study, the highest contents of selected perﬂuorinated acids were identiﬁed for the breakfast bag samples (2.54–6.60 pg/cm2), especially for B and C brands with the concentration of 6.60 and 5.35 pg/cm2, respectively, while the lowest content was noted in roasting bag samples (0.27–0.40 pg/cm2). The contents of perﬂuorinated sulfonates were inversed as compared with those of perﬂuorinated acids. The highest contents of selected perﬂuorinated sulfonates were found in roasting bag samples (1.38–5.17 pg/cm2), especially for B brand. The analysed perﬂuorinated sulfonates were not detected in any of the breakfast bag samples. The highest content of perﬂuorinated compounds were reported for B brand food contact materials. The data indicate a risk of food contamination by perﬂuorinated compounds from the commonly used food contact materials. Copyright © 2015 John Wiley & Sons, Ltd. Received 28 November 2014; Revised 17 April 2015; Accepted 11 May 2015 KEY WORDS: perﬂuorinated compounds (PFCs); PFCAs; PFASs; food contact materials; LC-MS/MS INTRODUCTION Perﬂuorinated acids (PFCAs) and perﬂuorinated sulfonates (PFASs) belong to the family group of perﬂuorinated compounds (PFCs). Because of the strength of the C–F bond, they are chemically very stable and highly resistant to biological degradation. Therefore, they belong to a class of compounds that tend to persist in the environment and are proposed as a new class of persistent organic pollutants.1 Within the class of PFCs, perﬂuorooctanoic acid (PFOA) and perﬂuorooctane sulfonate (PFOS) are generally considered reference substances.1 They have been most intensively studied from a toxicolog- ical standpoint as they are toxic, highly persistent and bioaccumulative. Human exposure to PFCs is due to a variety of environmental and product-related sources, although food (drinking water included) represents the dominant intake pathway. Also, dermal exposure may occur by skin contact with PFC-treated products. 4–6 Therefore, human exposure to PFCs is currently receiving considerable attention from scientist and policymakers because of the ubiquity of these substances in human blood and tissue samples worldwide, but particularly in industrialized areas. PFOA are one of the two PFCs that have been recently reported at ppb levels in serum of the general population in many countries. Perﬂuorinated compounds are routinely used in industry as stain–resistant and water–resistant coatings for fabrics, carpets and clothing; in ﬁreﬁghting foam; in cooking utensils as nonstick coatings; or in food contact material as a coating (e.g. baking paper to prevent oil leaching into the paper).7,8 When coming into contact with foods, paper and cardboard packaging is protected from softening by treatment with, among other things, water-resistant and oil-resistant perﬂuorochemicals. Contaminants in the coatings ﬂuorotelomer alcohols can be present, and ~1% of them can be converted to PFOA in the body.9,10 Furthermore, PFOA is used in the production of polytetraﬂuoroethylene nonstick surface coatings for cooking utensils or paper coatings, and therefore, it may be present in residual amounts.11 Powley et al.12 were unable to detect a migration of PFOA from PFTE-coated cooking utensils [limit of detection (LOD) 0.1 ng/cm2]. Begley et al.13 showed that nonstick cooking utensils contribute less to PFC exposure to food than coated papers or cardboard boxes. Residual amounts of PFOA in the range of a few nanograms per kilogram were all that could be detected in polytetraﬂuoroethylene cooking utensils. In contrast, some paper and cardboard surface coatings contained large amounts of PFCs. Microwave popcorn bags were found to contain 3–4 mg/kg (11 μg/dm2). There is evidence that migration was enhanced by a temperature increase.13 After heating, the PFOA concentration in the popcorn itself was about 300 μg/kg because it had migrated into the oil that coated the popcorn. Sinclair et al.14 examined the emission of residual PFOA and ﬂuorotelomer alcohols from nonstick cooking utensils and microwave popcorn bags upon heating to normal cooking temperatures (179–233°C surface temperature). Heating nonstick frying pans released 7–337 ng (0.11–5.03 ng/dm2) PFOA in the gas phase. Tittlemier et al.,15 in the Canadian Total Diet Study, analysed contamination with selected PFCs in various food products. The highest concentrations of perﬂuorooctane sulfonamide (maximum 27.3 μg/kg) were found in fast-food products that are foods that are commonly packaged in greaseproof paper. Similar to the results of Begley et al.,13 the US Food and Drug Administration named coated paper as the largest possible source of ﬂuorochemicals. According to the Food and Drug Administration, nonstick frying pans are, by comparison, an insigniﬁcant source of PFCs.16 As indicate earlier, there is a big risk of food contamination via PFC migration from food contact materials. It should be noted that the migration of a compound from a food contact material into food depends not only on PFC concentration level in the food contact material but also on the physico-chemical properties of the compounds and the food, as well as on the nature of materials used to manufacture the laminate.17 Several studies point out the possibility of underestimation of PFC exposure to food contact materials. As mentioned before, there is scarce information on PFC content in food contact materials like nonstick coatings on cookware or paper coating to provide oil and moisture to paper food contact material (e.g. popcorn paper bags). According to our knowledge, there is a lack of information regarding PFC contamination of commonly used food contact materials like wrapping paper and breakfast bags. Although there is evidence that migration of PFCs from food contact materials into food was enhanced by a temperature increase, available literature does not provide data concerning the contamination of PFCs for roasting bags. Therefore, there is a need to identify PFCs in a wide range of food contact materials as well as in food. Determination of PFCs occurring usually at low level is an analytical challenge. Up to now, there has been little investigation of the PFC levels for food contact materials mainly due to the difﬁculty in measuring these compounds by the conventional analytical technique used such as gas chromatography–mass spectrometry or liquid chromatography with UV detection (LC-UV). Many PFCs are not detectable by these methods, and only the development of LC-MS/MS methods made possible their measurement at low levels. The aim of this study was to demonstrate the presence of PFCs in the three brands of wrapping papers, breakfast bags, baking papers and roasting bags samples, using LC-MS/MS system. MATERIALS AND METHODS Chemicals Reagents in MS grade including acetonitrile, methanol and formic acid were purchased from Sigma Chemical Co., St. Louis, MO, USA. Water was puriﬁed with a Mili-Q system (Millipore, Bedford, MA, USA). Analytical standards of high purity: PFOA (96%) and PFOS (≥98%) powders, were obtained from Sigma-Aldrich Chemie GmbH, Germany. Native PFC Solution/Mixture (>98%) was obtained from Wellington Laboratories, Canada. Stock, intermediate and working standard solutions of PFCs at a concentration of 2 ng/ml were prepared in 20% MeOH (v/v) with 1% (v/v) of formic acid. Equipment The LC system (LC-20A, Shimadzu, Japan) consisted two pumps, an autosampler (set at 4°C), a column oven and a system controller coupled with mass spectrometer (QTRAP 5500, AB SCIEX, Canada) consisting a triple quadrupole, ion trap and ion source of electro-spray ionization and controlled by the ANALYST 1.5.1 software was used to perform the LC-MS/MS analyses. All chro- matographic determinations were performed on XBridge C18 150 × 2.1 mm 3.5 μm column (Waters, Milford, MA, USA) at 45°C with the ﬂow rate of 0.23 ml/min. Compounds were eluted in gradient system composed of water/formic acid (99.0/1.0, phase A) and acetonitrile/formic acid (99.0/1.0, phase B). Gradient was as follows: 30% B (0.0 min), 30–80% B (0.0–5.0 min), 80% B (5.0–20.0 min), 80–30% (20.0–21.0 min) and 30% (21.00–41.0 min). Qualitative and quantitative analyses were made using multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) method. Optimal identiﬁcation of compounds analysed was achieved under the following conditions: negative ionization, curtain gas: 25 l/min, collision gas: 9 l/min, ionspray voltage: 4500 V, temperature: 350°C, one-ion-source gas: 30 l/min, two- ion-source gas: 35 l/min, declustering potential: 30 ÷ 85 V, entrance potential: 10 V, collision energy: 10 ÷ 100 eV and collision cell exit potential: 10 ÷ 38 V. Rotary evaporator PVO 400 INGOS was used for sample evaporation. AccuTM Thermoblock (Labnet, Edison, NJ, USA) with nitrogen (Linde Gas, Poland) was used to evaporate the solvent and concentrate the extracts. MPW 350 R Centrifuge (MPW Med. Instruments, Poland) was used for sample preparation. Material characteristic Three different brands of wrapping papers, breakfast bags, baking papers and roasting bags samples, ob- tained from typical, commercially available food contact products in Poland were used. The brands were labelled as A, B and C, respectively. Properties of tested food contact materials were summarized in Table 1. Prior to PFCs determination in tested food contact materials had not been stored any food before.
