Introduction Per- and polyﬂuoroalkyl substances (PFASs) have been produced for the past 60 years in many consumer products, such as surface protectors in food containers, ﬁre-ﬁghting foams, fabric, carpets, paper and leather. PFASs are also used for industrial applications, such as to assist polymerisation when producing ﬂuorinated polymers, and are often CONTACT Virginie Boiteux virginie.boiteux@anses.fr © 2016 Informa UK Limited, trading as Taylor & Francis Group 706 V. BOITEUX ET AL. present in the semi-conductor and aviation industries (hydraulic ﬂuids) [1]. PFASs are characterised by a hydrophobic alkyl chain of varying length (typically C4–C16), in which all or most of the carbon–hydrogen bonds are replaced by carbon–ﬂuorine bonds, and a hydrophilic end group. This hydrophilic part deﬁnes the type of PFAS. Perﬂuoroalkyl acids (PFAAs) including perﬂuorocarboxylic acids (PFCAs) and perﬂuoroalkyl sulphonic acids (PFSAs) are the most widely studied PFAS due to their unique physicochemical properties, i.e. thermal and chemical stability. PFAAs have been detected in diﬀerent types of water [2,3,4], sediments [5] and sludges [6,7,8]. Several PFAAs are now con- sidered as persistent, bioaccumulative and toxic chemicals, and are regarded as poten- tial hazards to human health [9,10]. Intensive exposure to these compounds can aﬀect the liver, reproduction and development in humans [11]. It was reported that high PFOA serum levels may be associated with testicular, kidney, prostate and ovarian cancers in addition to non-Hodgkin lymphoma [12]. Fluorotelomers (FTs) are a subgroup of PFASs. They are partially ﬂuorinated, a small carbon–hydrogen chain (generally two carbons) linking the perﬂuorinated carbon chain to a functional group, such as sulphonate (FTSA), saturated carboxylic acid (FTCA), or unsaturated carboxylic acid (FTCUA). FTs are used in many industrial applications as surfactants or surface protection products, for example [13]. Due to their carbon–hydrogen chain, FTs can be subjected to degradation in the environment and metabolised to end-stage metabolites, such as PFCAs [14]. In many countries, the sources and exposure pathways of PFASs in the aquatic environment have been investigated. Recently, the eﬄuent of domestic and/or industrial wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) has been recognised as one of the principle contributors to PFASs found in natural waters [15,16,17,18]. However, studies on the fate and behaviour of PFASs in WWTPs have often been fairly inconclusive because the list of analysed PFASs was very limited and did not include precursors of perﬂuoroalkyl acids (PFAAs), such as FTs. Another source of substantial environmental contamination may be the use of aqueous ﬁlm-forming foams (AFFFs) [19]. Several AFFFs contain PFASs in order to decrease the surface tension of the air–AFFF interface. These types of AFFFs are used to extinguish hydrocarbon fuel ﬁres where a large volume of ﬂammable liquid and the potential for ﬁre exists, such as on military bases, airports, oil reﬁneries and ﬁreﬁghter training sites [20]. Historically, various PFCA-, PFSA- and FT-based derivatives have been added to the AFFF formulation. The PFSA-based derivatives in AFFFs were phased out after 2002 when 3 M – the only producer – ceased its global production of perﬂuorooctane sulphonate anion (PFOS)-based products. The next-generation AFFFs are based on a mixture of predominantly 6:2 and 8:2 FTs [21]. Up to now, few studies have reported the occurrence of PFASs in natural waters, soils and sediments via AFFF contamination [22,23,24,25,26,27]. Most of these studies have focused on the analysis of PFAAs, PFOS being the main compound quantiﬁed, followed by perﬂuorohexane sul- phonate anion (PFHxS). To the best of our knowledge, only two authors have investi- gated the occurrence of FTs in groundwater and soil from U.S. military bases where AFFFs were regularly used [26,28]. PFHxS, PFHxA and their C8 analogues were the most abundant PFAS detected in groundwater, followed by the PFAA precursor 6:2 FTSA. The aim of this study was to optimise several analytical methods for the determina- tion of multi-class PFASs in diﬀerent environmental matrices, namely water, sediment, soil and sludge. Diﬀerent extraction techniques were optimised and coupled to liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC–MS) analysis. A survey on the presence of INTERNATIONAL JOURNAL OF ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYTICAL CHEMISTRY 707 PFASs around sites using and/or producing then discharging large amounts of PFASs was carried out in order to evaluate and improve the performance of these analytical methods. The PFASs analysed include 11 PFCAs with a carbon chain length varying from C4 to C14, 5 PFSAs with a carbon chain length of C4, C6–C8 and C10 and 13 precursors: 3 FTSAs, 4 FTCAs, 3 FTUCAs, perﬂuorooctane sulphonamide (FOSA), 6:2 FT sulphonamide betaine (6:2 FTAB) and its synthetic intermediate or degradation product 6:2 FT sulpho- namide amine (M4). A surrogate parameter method previously published [29] was furthermore adapted to assess the potential presence of unknown PFAA precursors: water samples were exposed to hydroxyl radicals generated by the thermolysis of persulphate under alkaline pH conditions. Thus, PFAA precursors were oxidised and transformed into PFCAs of related perﬂuorinated chain length. The comparison between initial PFCA concentrations measured before oxidation, PFCA concentrations generated by target PFAA precursors and PFCA concentrations measured after oxidation, enabled us to estimate a percentage of unexplained PFCAs and, thus deduce the presence of unknown PFAA precursors. The optimised methods were applied to surface water, groundwater, sediment and soil samples taken from a training site using AFFFs. 2. Experimental 2.1.
