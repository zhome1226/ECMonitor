Introduction Poly- and perﬂuoroalkyl substances (PFASs) have been synthesised and widely used in diﬀerent industrial and commercial applications since the 1950s. Their chemical and thermal stability in addition to its hydrophobic and lipophobic nature, lead to highly useful properties in surfactants and polymers [1]. Polymer applications include textile stain and water repellents, grease-proof food-contact paper and other food contact materials (FCM) used for cooking. Surfactants applications include their use as an additive in surface treatment baths, and aqueous ﬁlm-forming foams used to extinguish ﬁres, among others [2]. The high-energy C-F bonds convert PFASs into non- biodegradable, highly persistent and bio-accumulative compounds when they contain long ﬂuorinated alkyl chains [3] and on the other hand, they are diﬃcult to degrade using conventional treatment methods [4,5]. These compounds have been regulated in the last decade [6]. Perﬂuoroctane sulfo- nate (PFOS) has been classiﬁed as a PBT (persistent, bioaccumulative and toxic) chemical, being included in the Stockholm Convention list of persistent organic pollutants (POPs) as well as in the European Directive 2013/39/EU, that consider PFOS as a priority substance in the ﬁeld of water policy [7]. Additionally, perﬂuorooctanoic acid (PFOA) has been recommended for listing as a POP under the Stockholm Convention, and CONTACT María P. PFASs have been detected in human serum at ng/mL levels [8,9], so investigating routes of human exposure are of paramount importance in order to protect human health. Humans are primarily exposed to PFASs through food and water, and to a lesser extent, via dust contact by ingestion and inhalation [10]. Application of PFASs and their precursors in FCMs during production and storage of food is a further source of human exposure to PFASs. Previous studies of fast food packaging such as paperboard, paper cups, baking paper, microwave popcorn bags or wrappers detected a wide range of PFASs including not only perﬂuoroalkyl carboxylates (PFCAs) and perﬂuoroalkylsulfo- nates (PFSAs) but also ﬂuorotelomer alcohols (FTOHs) and polyﬂuoroalkyl phosphate esters (PAPs) [11–16]. Relative abundance of PFASs varies among product types and studies, indicating diﬀerences in packaging formulations among countries. PFASs containing FCMs can contribute to indirect dietary exposure via migration into food. Few studies have investigated the migration of PFASs from FCMs into food simulants or food products. Food simulants and test migration conditions for plastics have been deﬁned [17]. However, correlations between food and food simulants for paper have not been well established [18]. Begley et al. [19] and Begley et al. [20] studied the migration of PFOA from microwave popcorn bags into a food oil (Mygliol) and the migration of other ﬂuorotelomers into the water, vinegar, ethanol, butter and oil. Migration of PFOA and FTOHs has been also reported by Müller et al. [21], indicating that migration into polar and non-polar liquids over- or, respectively, underestimates the migration into real food, and that Tenax® can be applied as a suitable food simulant especially at elevated temperatures. According to Gruber et al. [22], levels of PFOA in food items after the migration contact increased only slightly whereas FTOH levels increased signiﬁcantly, especially at baking temperatures, thus indicating a release of FTOH from high molecular mother compounds. Moreover, the authors suggested that these degradation reactions take place in the food matrix after the migration of the mother compounds. Migration of PFBA, PFHxA and PFOA as well as of several FTOHs from baking papers into Tenax® and other food items has been described by Schlummer et al. [23] and Fengler et al. [24] at several temperatures, showing again that the contribution of FTOH is much more important than that of PFCAs. Yuan et al. [25] studied the migration of FTOHs and PFCAs from paper bowls to food simulants such as water, ethanol and oil, reporting migration rates for PFOA ranging from 0.83% (into water) to 28.7% (into 50% ethanol). Trier et al. [10] reported the migration of PAPs from paper and board FCMs into a food simulant (ethanol 95%). However, studies based on food simulants indicate that common food simulants do not provide an accurate measure of the PFASs quantity that actually migrates into food [20]. Migration of PFCAs has been found dependent on paper characteristics, time and food simulant [26]. It has been also pointed out that PFASs have a strong preference for binding to proteins [27]. Furthermore, only one study has dealt with a complete range of PFCAs compounds [25]. This study aims to better understand potential exposure to PFSAs and PFCAs via FCMs by performing speciﬁc migration tests using a commercial paperbag packaging containing PFASs. Eleven PFCAs and ﬁve PFSAs were analysed by UPLC-MS/MS. Tenax® was used as food simulant in the migration tests. Whole and low-fat commercial milk INTERNATIONAL JOURNAL OF ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYTICAL CHEMISTRY 1425 samples, previously lyophilised, were also employed to investigate the potential migra- tion of PFASs to real foods. Migration tests were also carried out at high temperatures. The comparison between the migration of PFASs to simulant and to the real foods at the diﬀerent temperature conditions was also targeted in this work. 2. Experimental 2.1. Standards and reagents Two diﬀerent certiﬁed standard solutions were purchased from Wellington Laboratories (Guelph, Ontario, Canada): PFC-MXA and PFS-MXA, containing PFCAs and PFSAs, respec- tively, at individual concentrations of 2 μg/mL. A detailed list of the target analytes, acronyms, formulas and purities of the standards is given in Table 1. All solvents were UPLC-MS quality and Milli-Q water was used throughout. 2.2. FCM, simulant and food samples Paperbags for pet food packaging include an internal grease-proof paper, in addition to another external paper to be labelled with printing inks. The grammage of the internal brown paperbag was 72.9 g/m2, presenting a thickness of 110 μm. The experiments were carried out using only the raw homogeneous internal packaging material, which was used as a source of PFASs, before having any contact with pet food and printing inks. Tenax® (60–80 mesh particle size), poly(2,6-diphenyl-p-phenylene oxide), the standard food simulant for dry foods [17], was purchased from Sigma Aldrich. Whole and low-fat milk samples (Central Lechera Asturiana, Spain) were acquired from a local supermarket. Their fat content was 3.6% and 1.55% (w:v), respectively, and protein percentages were 3.1% and 3.15% (w:v), respectively. All milk samples were lyophilised (Telstar, LyoQuest). 2.3. Migration experiments Migration experiments were performed as follows: Paperbag samples were cut into pieces of 5 cm x 5 cm in size, and each piece was weighted. All the experiments were made in duplicate using pieces from the same bag and similar characteristics. The internal layer of the piece of paper was covered by a thin layer of the simulant Tenax® (or lyophilised milk), then wrapped into aluminium foil and introduced into a Petri dish previously heated, that was placed into the oven at the test temperature. Migration conditions for six months storage at refrigerated temperature were adjusted to 10 days at 40°C, following the European regulation on testing migration from FCM [17]. Moreover, migration experiments were also performed at 80°C, 120°C and 160°C during 2 h of contact between FCM samples and both Tenax® and milk samples. Afterwards, the food simulant or lyophilised milk samples were separated from the paperbag samples for extraction and analysis. All the experiments were carried out in duplicate and the fulﬁlment of the mass balance was checked by measuring the initial concentrations of PFASs in paperbag as well as the sum of those obtained both in residual paperbag and the simulant or food after the migration experiments. 1426 Table 1. Sample preparation PFASs were extracted from the tested FCM. After the contact, the 5 × 5 cm2 pieces of paperbag were separated from the layer of Tenax® and were cut into tiny pieces. The FCM samples, Tenax® and the lyophilised milk samples were separately mixed with 10 mL methanol in a rotary mixer for 24 h, followed by a second ultrasonic extraction for 20 min. The methanol extracts were then ﬁltered (0.22 μm) and transferred to a 15 mL polypropylene (PP) tube to be further evaporated until dryness under a gentle stream of dry nitrogen gas in a Turbo Vap. The ﬁnal sample volume was adjusted to 200 µL of Milli-Q water prior to injection. 2.5. Instrumental analysis and quantiﬁcation The sample extracts were analysed using an Ultrahigh Performance Liquid Chromatography (UHPLC) system (H-Class, Waters) coupled to a Triple Quadrupole Detector (TQD) (MS/MS, Waters, Milford, MA, USA) with an electrospray ionisation (ESI) interface operated in the negative ionisation mode [28]. A Waters BEH C18 column (50 mm x 2.1 mm x 1.7 µm) at 50°C was used for the analytical separation. The mobile phase consisted of Milli-Q water containing 2 mmol/L ammonium acetate and 5% methanol (A), and methanol (B). The operating ﬂow rate was 0.4 mL/min in gradient mode. The initial mobile phase composition was 75% of A during the ﬁrst half a minute of the analysis, which continued to reach 15% A at minute 5 and 100% B at minute 5.1 (hold time 0.5 min). Initial conditions were regained at 8 min followed by equilibration until 10 min. The detection was done in a multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) acquisition mode. Nitrogen was used as nebuliser and drying gas, and argon as the collision gas. Electrospray negative ionisation was carried out. A nitrogen gas ﬂow rate of 750 L/h and a drying gas temperature of 400°C were employed. Fragmentor voltages and collision energy were optimised for the diﬀerent target analytes. These results are listed in Table 1 together with the MS/MS transitions. Dilutions from the stock standard solutions were prepared in water at seven concentration levels ranging from 1 ng/mL to 250 ng/mL and calibration curves were built in order to calculate the PFASs concentrations in real samples and to control the linear range of the instrumental response. Analyte conﬁrmation criteria were deﬁned as the ion ratio for each compound obtained from PFASs standards analyses. The ion ratio was calculated by the Masslynx software for the precursor ion (Table 1) related to the secondary ion trace of each PFASs deﬁned in Table 1 as product ions. It was updated with every calibration curve. Furthermore, a tolerance criterion was given for the ion ratio of each compound and an ion retention window was set for the secondary ion trace. For the PFASs quantiﬁcation, the primary ion trace (Table 1) was employed. Values of LOD (µg/kg) are listed in Table 1. LODs were estimated as the lowest concentration of each PFAS compound in each matrix sample (paperbag, Tenax® and milk) giving a peak area equal to the blank signal plus three times the standard deviation of the blank. Quality control of the method was made using method blanks and calibration linearity. 1428 M. P. ELIZALDE ET AL. 3. Conclusions The grease-proof paperbag used in packaging for pet food market has shown to contain 10 perﬂuorocarboxylic acids (PFCAs) with diﬀerent lengths of the perﬂuoroalkyl carbon chain, in the series from PFBA (four carbon atoms) to PFTeDA (14 carbon atoms), being PFOA the most abundant. The perﬂuoroalkyl compounds did not migrate to the food simulant Tenax® after 10 days at 40°C, which after extrapolation indicates that no migration of PFCAs is expected after any storage time at refrigerated conditions. On one hand, although it can be assumed that migration decreases with the chain length, unexpected high migration percentages were observed for the long chain PFCAs whereas a maximum migration percentage for PFHxA was obtained between the short chain PFCAs. A similar trend has been reported by Still et al. [29] for the migration of PFCAs to butter at 5°C after 45 days. On the other hand, the migration of PFCAs at 80°C and 120°C into the milk samples is much higher than that obtained into Tenax® in the same conditions. Moreover, compar- ing migration percentages of the short chain compounds to the lyophilised whole and low-fat milk samples, it seems that migration increases with the fat content of the milk. In this sense, Tenax® has been conﬁrmed to be a suitable food simulant for dry foods with low and intermediate fat content such as semolina [30]. However, it has also been reported that Tenax® provides underestimation of the migration of some lipophilic analytes such as infant whole milk [29] or whole powdered milk at 75–121°C [31]. The results obtained in this work agree with previous results and call into question the adequation of Tenax® as a simulant for this type of food, at least at high temperatures.
